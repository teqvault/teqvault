The Factions of Eyuforyia: Designing Non-Humanoid Characters

A look into our character design philosophy — why we chose to omit humans entirely and focus on organic animal avatars to battle the Tech-Biomes, and how that one decision fundamentally changes how the player interacts with the world of Eyuforyia.

By Teq Vault Narrative Team • 9 Min Read

Shattering the Master/Pet Dynamic

The primary reason we chose to remove humans from this story is that we didn't want the creatures to be viewed as "pets" to be controlled by their masters. In most creature-collecting games, humans are the central figures — they are the ones collecting, building, strategizing, and commanding these characters in battle.

That dynamic creates a layer of separation. It turns living, breathing inhabitants of a world into mere tools or collectibles used to finish a human's story. Their victories aren't really their victories. Their losses aren't really their losses. They're just units on a board, waiting to be deployed by someone with a Pokédex and a dream.

In Eyuforyia, these characters are not fighting for a badge or a human's glory. They are fighting for their own land and their own survival. When Ice Heart — a glacier wolf with frost-tipped claws and the weight of grief driving every strike — charges across a hexagonal metal battlefield to rescue his captured brother, that is his mission. Not yours. You are him.

"The aim here is for the player to see themselves through the lens of our heroes, not like a master trying to use pets to finish a story."

— Teq Vault Narrative Design, For Land and Kin Story Bible

The World They're Defending: Eyuforyia

To understand why autonomy matters, you first have to understand what's at stake. Eyuforyia is a world of extraordinary, organic beauty — a place where the skies shimmer with violet and gold, where dew on giant clover leaves sparkles in perpetual twilight, and where every creature from the moss-covered pandas of Meadow-Core to the bioluminescent goats of the border forests has carved out a life shaped entirely by its own nature.

It is not a world built around human civilization. There are no cities designed for bipedal walking. There is no infrastructure built to human scale. The Azure Heights, Eyuforyia's proudest landmark, is a range of sapphire hills where gravity runs lighter than anywhere else — a feature that makes perfect biological sense for creatures that leap and glide and bound, and makes absolutely no provision for the kind of creature that walks upright and checks its phone.

This is the world the Tech-Biomes are eating. When the alien vessels anchor into the planet's crust and begin pumping neon-green corruption into the soil, when the soft azure grass withers and stiffens into razor-wire, when the wind's quiet song is replaced by the grinding thrum of machinery — that's not a backdrop. That's a home being destroyed. And the only ones who can stop it are the ones who live there.

Autonomy in the Face of the Tech-Biomes

By granting full autonomy to our non-humanoid roster, the stakes of the conflict immediately change. When Ice Heart faces down the Mirror of Frost and Steel — a nightmare wolf half-encased in chrome plating, its organic tail replaced by a segmented metal whip that throws blue electrical sparks — the conflict is deeply personal in a way that no human trainer standing at a safe distance could replicate.

Ice Heart isn't responding to a command prompt. He's watching his world get overwritten in real time, and his brother has been taken by machines that turn flesh into steel. When he roars "For Eyuforyia!" and leaps — only to be swatted aside like debris by a reinforced steel backhand — you feel it because it is his failure. His grief. His bruised ribs and his absolute refusal to stop getting back up.

Hotly, the firebird who saves him, doesn't arrive because a player tapped an ability button. She arrives because she was already watching. She was already afraid. She flew north alone when everyone else went back to sleep, and she saw the Azure Heights being dismantled, and she chose — on her own, with her own judgment — to do something about it even when it cost her everything she had.

That kind of character only works when the character is the protagonist. Not a tool. Not a companion. The protagonist.

The Factions: A Society Without Humans

Removing humans from the equation forced us to build something far more interesting: a genuinely non-human social ecosystem. Eyuforyia isn't a wilderness where animals roam without structure. It's a world with distinct civilizations, each shaped by the environment their creatures call home.

The Ground-Walkers of Meadow-Core operate on communal consensus, led by elders like the majestic stag Elder Hoof, whose living cherry-wood antlers are already in bloom when crisis arrives. The Sky Queties of the Cloud Sanctuary are detached philosophers, floating through crystal-spired cities and debating in musical tones — not because they're arrogant, but because they've genuinely never been given a reason to look down. Their queen, NyAqueen, doesn't process the world through emotion. She processes it through logic and variables, which means Ice Heart can only reach her by giving her one undeniable mathematical truth: a world made of metal needs no sky, only a ceiling.

The Magma-Pangolins of the Molten Core operate under the Law of the Forge, where only the strongest metal survives the hammer. They won't negotiate. They won't listen to warnings. The only language they respect is a strike heavy enough to crack obsidian — which creates one of the most satisfying moments in the entire story, when Ice Heart earns their respect not through diplomacy but through the specific, enormous sound of a glacier's worth of cold shattering a Magmian chest plate.

None of these social structures were designed around human intervention. They evolved naturally out of what these creatures are, what they fear, what they value, and what kind of power they respect. That's what becomes possible when you're not designing around a human protagonist who needs to understand and relate to everyone in the first act.

How This Translates into QUETIEMALS

This philosophy ripples through every aspect of our transmedia pipeline — including and especially the game. In QUETIEMALS, the match-3 combat grid isn't a human trainer calling out moves. It's the player inhabiting the perspective of a character who is fighting because they have to, because the alternative is watching their world become unrecognizable.

Every ability triggered on the board is designed to feel like a deliberate, self-determined strike rather than a commanded attack. Ice Heart's freeze mechanic isn't "freeze this tile" — it's the manifestation of a glacier wolf's bloodline magic, the same power he used (unsuccessfully) against the Mirror of Frost and Steel, now refined, now controlled, now aimed with the kind of intention that only comes from having something real to lose.

Hotly's thermal surge — a board-wide heat explosion that clears obstacles and deals area damage — is the same white-hot fire that pushed back a mechanized nightmare long enough for two battered heroes to escape into the sky. It's not a power fantasy. It's a memory.

When the player understands that these abilities are narrative in origin, not just mechanical, the game changes. You're not optimizing a roster. You're remembering who these creatures are and trusting them to do what they were born to do.

A World That Stands Alone

Ultimately, removing humans allowed us to build a more grounded, organic ecosystem. The heroes of Eyuforyia don't need a human protagonist to validate their struggle. They don't need a trainer to believe in them. They don't need a badge system to give their victories meaning.

Their world was beautiful before the ships came. Their world is worth defending because it's theirs. By stepping into their perspective, the player gets to experience a purer form of epic fantasy — one where the wild fights back on its own terms, with its own voice, for its own reasons.

That's the Eyuforyia we're building. And if you want to see it for yourself before the game releases, the world is already waiting in For Land and Kin — available now through the Teq Vault IP Library.
